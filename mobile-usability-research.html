Personal Loans Mobile Usability Research

"Reducing drop-off by improving trust, discoverability, and decision confidence in a high-stakes financial journey."

The Problem

Rising mobile sessions didn't improve conversions — users were researching on mobile but deciding on desktop.

Mobile traffic to the Personal Loan page was growing, but conversions remained flat. Analytics and stakeholder observations suggested friction in discoverability and navigation depth. Users were researching on mobile but finalising decisions on desktop, suggesting a confidence gap rather than a traffic issue.

Without clear comprehension, users risk abandoning the process, making poor financial decisions, or losing trust in the product. The study aimed to identify usability barriers and improve financial clarity and decision confidence.

Constraints

Mobile Screen Limitations

Multi-Step Navigation

Research Approach

Moderated sessions across 12 participants

Recruit

12 participants — mix of first-time and returning loan applicants, mobile-first users in Singapore markets.

Moderate

1 hour sessions using think-aloud protocol. Tasks focused on finding, comparing, and initiating a loan application.

Synthesize

Affinity mapping across sessions, prioritised by frequency and severity, cross-referenced against drop-off analytics.

Insights

Six patterns shaped the recommendations

01

Marketplace-style filtering expected

"I just want to filter by amount and see what I actually qualify for."

Users expected product logic, not sales logic.

02

Fold hierarchy caused distraction

"There's so much going on — I don't know where to look first."

Visual hierarchy needed a single focal point per screen.

03

Promotional language reduced trust

"'Deals For You' feels like an ad. I'm not sure it's real."

Marketing framing backfired in a high-stakes context.

04

Approval calculator increased confidence

"Oh — if I can see my chances, I'm more likely to apply."

Confidence signals directly influenced application intent.

05

Navigation fragmentation increased effort

"I had to go back and forth three times to compare two loans."

Cognitive load from architecture, not content volume.

06

Recommendation engine lacked transparency

"Why is this loan recommended for me? I don't see why."

Unexplained logic felt opaque and untrustworthy.

Decision Framework

Translating insights into strategic recommendations

Each recommendation is shown alongside a mobile screen prototype illustrating the proposed change.

Remove “More Details” Drawer

Why

Fragmented navigation across multiple interaction layers raised cognitive load and caused users to lose context mid-comparison.

Impact

Simpler information architecture with one clear reading path per loan card.

All loan details visible inline — no drawer navigation required

Reframe “Deals For You” to “Featured Offers”

Why

Promotional framing triggered marketing skepticism in a high-stakes financial context where users sought neutral guidance before rewards.

Impact

Increased trust perception by shifting to profile-based language over sales language. Reduces ad blindness and signals the content is platform-curated, not external Google ads.

Elevate Approval Calculator

Why

Users valued confidence signals over feature lists, yet the calculator was buried below multiple scroll depths.

Impact

Improved application confidence and intent-to-complete by surfacing the loan estimator above the fold.

Improve Recommendation Transparency

Why

Unexplained ranking logic felt opaque — users couldn't tell if recommendations served their interest or the platform's.

Impact

Built credibility by surfacing qualification criteria alongside each recommended product.

What we learned

The core problem was not usability alone. The deeper issue was trust architecture within a financial decision journey.

Reflection

"In fintech, the emotional layer of a decision is as important as the information layer. Designing for trust is not a soft concern — it is the primary UX lever in high-stakes financial products."
